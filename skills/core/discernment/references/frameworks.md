# Discernment: Source Frameworks

Deeper notes on the bodies of work behind the discernment framework. The `SKILL.md` is the operational tool; this file is the grounding — what each framework actually claims, where it comes from, and where it is contested. Read it when a user pushes on a claim, when you want a verifiable citation, or when you need to know the limits of a lens before leaning on it.

**A note on provenance.** The four lenses are presented in the parent skill as a tidy quartet. They are not. Three rest on peer-reviewed research (Dunning-Kruger, Tetlock, the BIT calibration study); one rests on a commercial assessment instrument (Hogan); and the SCQuARE framing rests on a branded consulting methodology with a well-documented academic ancestor (Minto). Where a claim is solid, this file says so. Where attribution is fuzzy or popularised beyond its evidence, this file says that too. Calibration applies to the framework itself, not only to the decisions it examines.

## Step 1 — SCQuARE (problem definition)

**What it is.** A structured way to force crisp problem definition before analysis: Situation → Complication → Question → Answer, extended in the parent skill with **R**ationale and **E**vidence.

**Lineage — be precise about this.** The S-C-Q-A core is **Barbara Minto's**, developed at McKinsey & Company in the late 1960s–1970s and published in *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking* (Minto, 1987; widely revised). Minto was McKinsey's first female MBA hire (1963) and also coined MECE. SCQA is the situational opening of her pyramid: a stable Situation, a Complication that disrupts it, the Question that follows, and the Answer that becomes the top of the pyramid.

"SCQuARE" specifically is a **branded commercial thinking-and-planning methodology** (SCQuARE International) that extends Minto's SCQA with additional steps. The "Rationale and Evidence" tail used in this skill reflects that extension. Treat the *technique* as well-founded (it is just Minto's structured problem statement) and the *acronym* as a trademark, not an academic citation. I could not verify a single canonical originator for the "RE" extension beyond the SCQuARE consultancy.

**Why it earns its place as Step 0.5.** Minto's insight is that most flawed analysis is not bad reasoning — it is correct reasoning aimed at the wrong question. Naming the Situation and Complication separately stops the two most common failures: acting on a Complication nobody actually agrees is true, and answering a Question that was never the real one.

**Key reference.** Minto, B. *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking.* (Pearson/FT, multiple editions.)

## Lens 1 — Dunning-Kruger

**Source.** Kruger, J., & Dunning, D. (1999). "Unskilled and Unaware of It: How Difficulties in Recognizing One's Own Incompetence Lead to Inflated Self-Assessments." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 77*(6), 1121–1134.

**The core claim, in their words:**

> "the skills that engender competence in a particular domain are often the very same skills necessary to evaluate competence in that domain — one's own and anyone else's."

And the consequence:

> "those with limited knowledge in a domain suffer a dual burden: Not only do they reach mistaken conclusions and make regrettable errors, but their incompetence robs them of the ability to realize it."

**What this means for discernment.** The effect is fundamentally about **metacognition**, not raw error. The dangerous state is not ignorance (people who know nothing usually know they know nothing) but the early-competence zone where someone knows just enough to feel sure. That is why the operational question in the skill — *"if you turned out to be wrong, what would be the most likely reason?"* — works: people in the trap cannot generate a credible answer, because the same gap that produces the error hides it.

**Caveat — and you should know this if challenged.** The original effect has been seriously contested on statistical grounds. Critics (e.g., Krueger & Mueller, 2002; Nuhfer et al.; and various reanalyses) argue that much of the classic "chart" is explained by **regression to the mean** and **the better-than-average effect** combined with measurement error, and that the autocorrelation in plotting self-assessment error against skill can manufacture the pattern. The metacognitive interpretation survives in weakened form; the crisp "incompetent people are maximally overconfident" story is shakier than its pop-culture status implies. Use the lens as a prompt to check expertise honestly, **not** as a proof that a confident person is incompetent — that inference does not hold.

## Lens 2 — Hogan Judgment

**Source.** A commercial assessment from **Hogan Assessment Systems**, not a single paper. Primary write-up: *Hogan Perspective: Judgment* (Hogan Assessment Systems, 2014); see also the Hogan Judgment Report and hoganjudgment.com.

**What the instrument actually measures.** The Judgment assessment combines (1) brief cognitive-reasoning measures — a timed numerical section and a timed verbal section; (2) three non-cognitive **decision-making style** scales; and (3) an assessment of **post-decision reactions**, especially responses to negative feedback. The three style scales — confirmed against Hogan's own materials — are:

| Scale | Poles | What each pole describes |
|---|---|---|
| Risk orientation | **Threat-avoidant ↔ Reward-seeking** | Threat-avoidant decision-makers focus on the downside and minimise potential losses; reward-seekers focus on the upside and maximise potential gains. |
| Time/scope horizon | **Tactical ↔ Strategic** | Tactical thinkers are detail-oriented and focused on immediate needs; strategic thinkers focus on the big picture and long-term plans. |
| Information use | **Data-driven ↔ Intuitive** | Data-driven decision-makers review relevant facts and revisit past decisions as data updates; intuitive decision-makers rely on accumulated experience and move quickly. |

(The parent skill labels these "Motivation," "Thinking style," and "Information processing." Those are reasonable plain-language relabels of Hogan's Threat/Reward, Tactical/Strategic, and Data-driven/Intuitive scales.)

**The genuinely distinctive part.** Most decision models stop at the moment of choice. Hogan's adds what happens *after* a decision fails: does the person engage with the negative feedback and update, or deny, deflect, and rationalise? Hogan's framing is that the decision process "is only completed after the leader evaluates the outcome." This is the most useful element for discernment, because it predicts whether a bad call will be a one-off or a pattern.

**Caveat.** This is a proprietary instrument with the usual caveats of commercial personality assessment — neither pole is "better," and the value is in naming a tendency, not labelling a person. You do not need the assessment to use the lens: ask which pole the reasoning leans toward and what that pole habitually underweights. Hogan also positions Judgment as integrating its "bright-side" (HPI) and "dark-side" (HDS) personality models with core values; the four-dimension version in the skill is a simplification, which is fine for a thinking tool.

## Lens 3 — Mark Egan / Behavioural Insights Team (calibration)

**Source.** Egan, M. (2024). *The UK public is overconfident.* Behavioural Insights Team, Working Paper No. 007 (August 2024). Dr. Mark Egan is a Principal Research Advisor at BIT.

**The headline numbers (from the BIT study of 2,000+ UK adults).** Participants rated confidence on 30 general-knowledge true/false statements:

- Average confidence: **83%**. Average accuracy: **68%**. The gap *is* the overconfidence.
- **81%** of respondents were overconfident (confidence exceeded accuracy by ≥5 points).
- Only **~15%** were well-calibrated; **~3%** were underconfident.
- Overconfidence rose with age in the sample (boomers most overconfident at ~84%, Gen Z least at ~75%).

The skill's claim that "80%+ of UK adults are more confident than they are correct" maps directly to the 81% figure. **Well-calibrated** is defined as confidence tracking accuracy — the rare and valuable state.

**The debiasing tools — with honest attribution.** The skill bundles these under Egan's "good form for thinking" toolkit. Two of them have older, separate origins worth knowing:

- **Premortem.** Originated by **Gary Klein** ("Performing a Project Premortem," *Harvard Business Review*, September 2007; see also Klein, *Sources of Power*, 1998). The technique: assume the decision has already failed and work backwards to explain why. It is prospective hindsight, and it reliably surfaces risks that forward-looking planning suppresses. BIT and Egan advocate it; they did not invent it.
- **Thinkgroup / dissenting voice.** The functional idea — deliberately seat someone who genuinely disagrees before finalising — is the well-established **red-team / structured-dissent** practice (and the antidote to Janis's *groupthink*, 1972). I could not verify "thinkgroup" as a specific coined term from Egan; treat it as a label for structured dissent rather than a citable construct.
- **Confidence diagnostic and "cognitive hygiene"** are framing devices for the calibration message; use them, but don't present them as named, sourced instruments.

**Why this lens is the spine of the framework.** It supplies the actual goal — calibration, not confidence reduction — and the only honest test of it: *how often were you right when you previously felt this sure?* Confidence numbers without a track record are noise.

## Lens 4 — Tetlock / Superforecasting

**Sources.**
- Tetlock, P. (2005). *Expert Political Judgment: How Good Is It? How Can We Know?* Princeton University Press.
- Tetlock, P., & Gardner, D. (2015). *Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction.* Crown.
- The **Good Judgment Project** (2011–2015), the team that won IARPA's forecasting tournament.

**The famous finding.** In the Expert Political Judgment work (≈284 experts, ≈28,000 predictions), the average expert was, in Tetlock's now-canonical phrasing, **roughly as accurate as a "dart-throwing chimpanzee."** The deeper result was the split *within* the experts, borrowed from Isaiah Berlin's essay *The Hedgehog and the Fox* (1953):

- **Hedgehogs** know "one big thing," reason from a single grand theory, are confident, and forecast *worse* than chance on hard questions.
- **Foxes** know "many little things," stitch together multiple perspectives, hold loose probabilistic views, and forecast better.

In the Good Judgment Project, trained **superforecasters outperformed intelligence-community analysts who had access to classified material by roughly 30%.** Crucially, this was attributable to *process and habits*, not IQ — which is why the skill insists the lens is about whether reasoning is structured and updatable, not whether the person is smart.

**The superforecaster habits (operationalised).** Drawn from the "Ten Commandments" appendix of *Superforecasting*:

- **Take the outside view first.** Establish the base rate before reasoning about the specifics. The inside view always makes your case feel exceptional; it usually isn't.
- **Fermi-ise.** Break a hard question into estimable sub-questions.
- **Use precise probabilities, not verbal fog.** "70%," not "likely." Precision is what makes a forecast scorable, and scorability is what makes calibration improvable.
- **Update incrementally (Bayesian).** Move in proportion to the evidence's actual weight — under-updating and over-updating are both errors.
- **State what would change your mind**, in advance. A belief with no disconfirming condition is an identity, not a forecast.
- **Keep score.** Track not just whether you were right but how right you were relative to how sure you were (Brier scores).

**Caveat.** Superforecasting evidence is strongest for **resolvable, medium-term geopolitical/economic questions** with clean ground truth. For one-shot, deeply novel, or unresolvable decisions (most leadership and people calls), the *habits* transfer but the *track-record machinery* does not — you often can't score the outcome cleanly. Use the discipline; don't over-promise the precision.

## Cross-framework summary

| Lens | The question it forces | The failure it catches |
|---|---|---|
| SCQuARE | Are we answering the right question? | Excellent analysis aimed at the wrong problem. |
| Dunning-Kruger | How deep is the expertise, really? | Familiarity mistaken for competence. |
| Hogan | What does this decision style underweight — and will they update if it fails? | Predictable blind spots; non-coachability. |
| Egan / BIT | Is the confidence calibrated to a track record? | Overconfidence, the dominant bias. |
| Tetlock | What's the base rate, and what would change the view? | Inside-view bias; unfalsifiable conviction. |

The lenses overlap deliberately. Calibration (Egan) is the goal; Tetlock supplies the process to reach it; Dunning-Kruger and Hogan name two distinct sources of the gap (missing metacognition, and stylistic blind spots); SCQuARE makes sure all of it is pointed at the right target.
